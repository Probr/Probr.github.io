Use-Cases

In order to evaluate the usability of the probr system, the system was tested with a specific use case. The test run took place at a university meeting room (around 35 square meters area), where a scientific project meeting of 20 to 25 persons took place for two consecutive days. We will call this use case 'acelab' (after the room it took place in).

During the meeting, we set-up six sniffing devices to collect WiFi probe requests. The devices used for sniffing were Odroid C1 devices with the standard Odroid WiFi Module 4.

Research Questions

The data collected at mentioned meeting were used to showcase and evaluate the probr system and answer the following research questions:

Q1: Room Utilization

Is it possible to determine room utilization, which means to determine the number of people in a room, using only passive wifi tracking?

Q2: Tracking

Can we track individual MAC addresses / persons ? What patterns are visible?

Q3: Device Statistics

What information can we get about the devices and vendors present during the examined period?

In the following, we will examine each of those questions and discuss how probr answers them. The user navigation in the probr-analysis application pays attention to the use cases we had in mind. Under the 'analysis' tab in the probr-analysis front-end, the user is presented with the tools to explore the collected data and answer the posed questions. In the navigation bar, the user has the possibility to define the "use case" or "experiment" by setting a tag and a date/time range globally. The defined use case is then applied to all the analysis views. More info on the front-end can be found here.

Q1: Room Utilization

After the development of probr-core and the first steps into the probr-analysis application, we noticed that just looking at the probe count or packet frequency is not enough to determine room utilization. Packets might end up being collected by our sniffing devices even if the device that originally sent out the packets just walked by shortly and didn't even enter the monitored room at all. Hence we had to think about a more sophisticated data model that we could construct from the packet data, in order to be able to say when someone has been present at a certain point in time and to determine how many MAC addresses or persons have been present at a given time t. This lead us to the definition of sessions, which proved to be a powerful tool for use cases and questions such as the one for Q1.

Under the 'utilization' tab in the 'analysis' part of the probr-analysis front-end, the utilization graph is displayed. It displays the number of concurrent sessions at each point in time.

The time axis granularity at which the concurrent session counts are computed is at 5 minute slots. Which means, the bar chart produces a bar for every five minutes on the time ranges, where the height of the bar is equal to the number of concurrent sessions present at that time slot. So this graph shows us, how many unique MAC addresses or devices have been present at different times in a room (defined by the tag).

One can clearly identify a pattern in the utilization graph. In order to be able to evaluate the correctness and accuracy of probrs utilization results, we also recorded the real number of persons in the room during the acelab. The graphic below compares both data sets.

The comparison shows that the utilization computed by the probr system is satisfying and fairly accurate. The breaks and starting or end times of the meeting days can be observed very clearly. It is important to note that the number of sessions will always be higher than the number of real persons at each point in time, since one person normally has multiple devices with them (e.g. one notebook and one smartphone) which will result in two sessions at the same time for that one person. But nevertheless, the patterns are detected, and one can conclude that probr does a good job answering room utilization questions.

In addition to the utilization of a room equipped with sniffing devices, probr allows its users to also look at the geographical locations or positions of the sniffed packets and devices. The locations are computed with a triangulation-based approach, as shown here. It provides the user with the ability to explore the geographic distribution of devices or persons in the monitored room over a certain period of time. The location view can be found under the 'location' tab in the analysis view. Below, we see a summary of the location heatmaps for the acelab experiment:

The heatmap generally is very similar to the actual positions of the participants in the acelab experiment during the experiment period. The upper part of the room (see figure above) was mainly used during the presentations at the meeting and hence the participants spent most of their time in that region. Occasionally, participants spread across the lower parts of the room during coffee breaks.

The use of WiFi probe requests for positioning has been used outdoors in many previous scientific works about WiFi sniffing and privacy. When it comes to indoors location, we come to the conclusion that computing locations via WiFi probe requests is only satisfying if the room / area that is monitored is rather large and the movements of the tracked devices are irregular. A small room introduces problems with accurracy, signal strength and obstacles. If you have participants that are very uniform in movement the heatmaps will not notice smaller changes to the heatmap of the room.
